\begin{abstract}
\noindent Education is the necessary and sufficient cause of human
development. No country has
developed without it. No country with sustained educational expansion
has failed to develop. The mechanism is biological: humans have the
longest juvenile dependency of any species --- nearly two decades of a
plastic brain embedded with a dedicated teacher. Extended juvenile
dependency is the protocol; education is the most powerful payload ever
delivered through it. Educated parents transmit knowledge, norms, and
health behaviours to their children, who exercise these as household
decisions \textasciitilde25 years later --- the time-to-agency interval
set by juvenile dependency itself. A great-grandparent's school
completion predicts their great-grandchild's life expectancy three
generations later; income's predictive power collapses within one.
Income, once education's contribution is removed, predicts nothing.
Provision without educated populations produces no durable outcomes.
Institutions are built by educated populations, not the reverse. By 2022, 154
countries have crossed both development thresholds (TFR below 3.65, life
expectancy above 69.8 --- the 1960 United States values) --- every one
through education. The evidence is a 185-country panel (1950--2015)
tested across three generational depths, with natural experiments that
no other development input survives.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} fundamental cause; parental transmission;
residualization; natural experiments; categorical literacy;
extended juvenile dependency; development threshold
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{introduction}

Education is fundamental to human well-being. Not important ---
fundamental. The distinction matters. Important means high on a list.
Fundamental means the list does not exist without it.

Education is the necessary and sufficient cause of human development.
No country has developed without it. No country with sustained
educational expansion has failed to develop --- but sufficiency operates
across generations, not within individual lifetimes. Educated people are
developed --- they live longer, have fewer children, make better health
decisions, and transmit these gains to their children without requiring
income or provision as intermediaries. The mechanism is Parental
Transmission (PT): an educated parent transmits knowledge, norms, health
behaviours, and the decision to educate the next child. PT carries the
gains forward automatically. Population-level development requires the
state to extend this to the whole population --- not to make education
work, but to make uneducated people educated. The state provides reach;
the household provides the mechanism.

The evidence is a 185-country panel (WCDE v3, 1950--2015) tested across
three generational depths (\textasciitilde25, \textasciitilde50,
\textasciitilde75 years), with natural experiments that no other
development input survives.

Why has this been missed? Every framework in development economics ---
income-first, institutions-first, provision-first, governance-first ---
was built by people standing on educational foundations they could not
see. Adam Smith (1776) observed the pin factory and built a theory of wealth
from the division of labour. He never asked where the educated workforce
came from. Scotland's Education Act of 1696 had been producing educated
Scots for eighty years before Smith was born; he took educated labour as
given --- the same structural blindness that makes education invisible to
educated populations today
(Sections~\ref{why-education-is-invisible}
and~\ref{completion-as-the-operative-variable}).
Japan, with negligible fossil fuels, industrialised within a generation
of the Meiji education reforms; Nigeria, sitting on one of the world's
largest oil reserves, did not. Every major development framework has made the
same structural move: mistaking the products of education for its causes.
The policy consequence is dispersion: resources spread across
interventions, with education funded as one line item rather than
recognised as the mechanism that makes the others work.

Others have seen parts of this, from two directions. In development
economics, Haq \& Sen created the Human Development Index (UNDP 1990),
breaking the identification of development with income. Sen's
capabilities framework (1999) showed that what matters is what people can do
and be, not what they earn --- and he saw the right anomaly: Kerala,
Sri Lanka, China, with welfare outcomes far above what their incomes
predicted. Easterlin (1981) identified mass schooling as the binding
constraint. Lutz \& Kebede (2018) showed the income--mortality relationship is tighter
when education replaces income on the x-axis: what looked like ``wealth
buys health'' was education proxying through income all along.

In evolutionary biology, Konner (2010) documented the longest juvenile
dependency period in any species --- roughly 18 years of dedicated
learning embedded with a dedicated teacher. Boyd \& Richerson (1985)
formalised cultural transmission through parent-to-child channels as the
mechanism by which human populations accumulate knowledge across
generations. Hawkes et al.\ (1998) showed that human post-reproductive
lifespans --- unique among primates --- evolved to support grandchild
survival, making longevity and fertility two expressions of the same
biological architecture.

These traditions --- one asking what development is, the other asking
what humans are --- converge in this paper's central claim: that
education is the cause, income and provision are downstream, and the
mechanism operates through the biological channel of parental
transmission across generations. This paper states it and tests it.

Section~\ref{what-humans-are} develops the mechanism. Section~\ref{defining-development} defines development.
Section~\ref{the-evidence} presents the evidence. Section~\ref{the-cases} traces the mechanism through
the canonical cases. Section~\ref{the-decision} addresses the policy decision.

\section{What Humans Are}\label{what-humans-are}

\subsection{The Generational Transmission Mechanism}\label{the-generational-transmission-mechanism}

Educated parents pass what they know to their children, who pass it to theirs. This is the mechanism.

\textbf{PT (Parental Transmission)} is cultural
transmission through the household. The primary channel is parent to child,
but the household includes grandparents, older siblings, and extended kin
--- anyone co-resident during the juvenile dependency period. The channel
is biological, but what travels through it is cultural. The paper measures
PT through education completion; what the household actually transmits is
broader --- norms, aspirations, health behaviours, the decision to send
the next child to school. PT is the base unit, and it runs on
self-interest: household members invest in their own children without
requiring ideology, altruism, or institutional commitment to sustain the
behaviour. It transmits across generations: the grandchild's attainment
reflects the grandparent's through two sequential cycles
(\textasciitilde50 years), the great-grandchild's through three
(\textasciitilde75 years).
Of everything PT transmits, one item is operative: the decision to
educate the next child. A parent who completed school sends their child
to school --- with expectation, monitoring, and reinforcement. A parent
who did not, does not --- or does so without any of these. Completion
transmits as a norm. The completion threshold is the gate; what happens
after the gate is dose-response
(Section~\ref{completion-as-the-operative-variable}).
PT explains persistence --- why educational gains never depreciate. But
persistence alone does not produce development outcomes. What matters is
what education creates in the individual: \emph{categorical literacy}
--- the reorganisation of the brain that literacy and numeracy physically
produce (Dehaene et al.~2010; Section~\ref{completion-as-the-operative-variable}),
giving the capacity for planning, health-oriented decision-making, and
information processing that does not develop without schooling.
This capacity is exercised
through household decisions: fertility, health, investment, and
children's schooling. The \textasciitilde25-year
lag between educational investment and development outcomes is the
interval for educated children to become adults exercising these
decisions. Taiwan's development crossing arrived \textasciitilde20 years
after educational expansion began: one generation. Kerala's
arrived at \textasciitilde65 years: three generations of slow
accumulation before enough of the population was educated for household
decisions to produce population-level development outcomes. A woman
completing lower secondary at age 15--18 has children reaching school
age 20--30 years later.
Educational expansion has two parts. PT provides durability: it
transmits what parents already have. State investment provides reach: it
brings first-generation learners into the system and lifts the level
beyond what parents alone would transmit. Where the state invests, both
channels operate together --- parents transmit what they have while the
state reaches children whose parents have nothing (Section~\ref{education-vs-gdp-as-predictors-of-attainment}). Where
the state does not invest, PT alone preserves what the parent already
has --- nearly perfectly, but nothing more.

PT is the mechanism in every case; what varies is the state's
relationship to it. \textbf{No priority}: the state does not invest. Pre-industrial, this
produced stasis; post-industrial, educated minorities with surplus and
widened identity (Section~\ref{from-action-to-talk-how-education-reaches-beyond-the-household}) slowly expanded access through religious
and community channels, but never singular commitment (Section~\ref{the-decision}).
\textbf{Competing priority}: the state builds schools, but education
competes with health, nutrition, security, and governance.
\textbf{Singular priority}: the state makes education the unconditional
focus and builds ahead of need. The cases (Section~\ref{the-cases}) develop all three
regimes.

PT's durability reflects a
biological channel. Humans have plastic brains, but plasticity alone
explains nothing: a plastic brain in isolation learns nothing useful.
What delivers knowledge to the plastic brain is the human juvenile
dependency period --- roughly 18 years, compared to 8 for chimpanzees,
the longest in any species (Konner 2010). A dedicated learner embedded
with a dedicated teacher for nearly two decades: long enough for
cumulative cultural knowledge to be absorbed deeply enough to become the
learner's own baseline. The learner then becomes the teacher.
Plasticity is the hardware; the extended dependency is the protocol; the
parent is the carrier (Boyd \& Richerson 1985).

The educated household member --- parent or older sibling --- is not one
transmission channel among many. They are the switch that activates all
the others. Sending a child to school places that child in reach of
teachers, textbooks, peers, and every institution the educated world
has built. Without the decision, none of these fire. In many households
it is the first sibling to complete school who flips the switch ---
tutoring younger children, setting expectations, demonstrating that
completion is normal. Extended families, communities, and diasporas
amplify the signal. But the first educated member of a household is the
node through which every other channel reaches the next generation.

This architecture explains three properties. Non-depreciation: education
persists for life because what survives is not content but the cognitive
reorganisation that content exposure produced
(Section~\ref{completion-as-the-operative-variable}). Amplification: when the state builds
schools, it loads additional knowledge onto the same channel --- the
$\beta_g > 1$ result, where $\beta_g$ is the ratio of the child
generation's education gain to the parental baseline
(Section~\ref{education-vs-gdp-as-predictors-of-attainment}). Catastrophic reset: because the
knowledge lives in the teacher, not the institution, destroying the
educated population leaves the next generation's plastic brains with
nothing to absorb. Cambodia (Section~\ref{the-cases}) is this test: schools rebuilt
within a decade, progress stalled for a generation. Knox did not invent
parental transmission; he institutionalised it, channelling formal
schooling through a mechanism as old as the species.

The human baseline --- the state before formal education reaches a
population --- is high fertility, high child mortality, low life
expectancy, hunger, and disease. This is not a description of poverty.
It is a description of the species in the absence of the one
intervention that changes all of them simultaneously. Hunger and
insecurity are part of the baseline, not preconditions that must be
resolved before education can begin. Every successful educational
expansion in the historical record was made through poor nutrition and
insecurity, not after resolving them (Section~\ref{the-cases}).

The pathway runs: education restructures cognition; restructured
cognition restructures intent (family size becomes a choice, child
survival becomes an expectation); the fertility transition follows;
fewer children releases resources per child; those resources amplify the
next generation's education through PT; the cycle repeats. Each pass
raises the baseline.

\subsection{From Action to Talk: How Education Reaches Beyond the Household}\label{from-action-to-talk-how-education-reaches-beyond-the-household}

Education's effects start in the household but do not stay there.

The PT mechanism operates through action --- the household decisions of
educated adults. But education's effects extend beyond the household,
and the nature of that extension changes as the radius widens. Two
things widen it simultaneously: education expands the boundary of who
counts as ``my people'' --- coalitional psychology (Tooby \& Cosmides
2010) allows flexible group boundaries that education stretches from kin
to nation --- and it releases surplus, as educated parents have fewer
children and nearly all survive, freeing time, money, and attention.
The arc widens (who you invest in) and the constraint loosens (what you
can invest).

The result is four radii of effect, each weaker than the last:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Household.} PT is action: the parent raises the child in
front of them, and the 18-year biological channel guarantees delivery.
Runs on self-interest.

\item \textbf{Kin and identity.} Investment is still concrete and
compounding. Runs on self-interest extended by expanded identity.

\item \textbf{Beyond identity.} Education becomes talk --- advocacy,
policy campaigns, diluted across every good cause simultaneously.
This is where the competing-priority pathway
(Section~\ref{the-generational-transmission-mechanism}) originates.

\item \textbf{Global.} External provision --- vaccines, aid, health
delivery --- funded by the surplus of already-developed populations.
Talk, diluted further across borders and competing claims.
\end{enumerate}

The axis that predicts durability is motivational: PT compounds because
it runs on self-interest. Every non-education intervention requires
someone to keep serving past the point where self-interest would redirect
them. Revolutionary commitment, donor altruism, state fiscal discipline:
all are fragile because all run against the grain of normal human
motivation. Education runs with it. Non-education intervention buys
time; educational investment changes trajectory.

\subsection{Why Education Is Invisible}\label{why-education-is-invisible}

An educated person is physically indistinguishable from an uneducated
one. Same body, same biological drives, same appearance. Wealth builds
visible infrastructure; disease marks the body; education rewires
cognition and leaves no trace on the surface. The only observable signal
is behavioural change --- and we misread it. We see smaller families and
call it wealth flows, or contraceptive access, or patriarchy relaxing, or
telenovelas on television. We see longer lives and call it health systems.
We see rising productivity and call it economic growth. Every discipline
theorises the downstream behaviour without tracing it back to the
cognitive reorganisation that produced it. Education has no physical
signature, so the signature it does leave --- changed decisions, across
every domain, for the rest of the person's life --- is assigned to
whatever domain notices it first.

Educated people rank education as important --- one of many important
things, alongside health, nutrition, governance, and security. They
cannot categorise it as \emph{fundamental} because that would require
seeing their own cognition from outside. Uneducated people cannot value
it at all: the cost is immediate, the return is abstract, delayed by a
generation, and has no precedent in the household's experience. The
people who could identify education as fundamental cannot, because they
are inside it; the people who would benefit most cannot, because they
are outside it. Subjective wellbeing confirms the
blindspot: across 147 countries, self-reported happiness tracks income,
not education --- people feel richer, not more educated, so they
attribute progress to wealth.

This applies to the frameworks through which this paper will be
evaluated. Development as multivariate, education as one input among
many --- these are themselves products of the mechanism the paper
describes. The capacity to rank, compare, and weigh competing inputs is
itself a product of education. The training that makes ``education is
sufficient'' sound like overreach assumes a multivariate world. The
evidence will show that the other variables are either products of
education or irrelevant once education is present.

The historical exceptions confirm the rule. Within the development
frame, education competes with health, nutrition, governance, and
security in a cost-benefit comparison --- and it always loses, because
its returns are generational while the others' are immediate and
visible. A child dying of diarrhoea today is an emergency; a child not
sent to school today is no one's emergency --- until her child arrives
as one in 2050. Every
historical breakthrough bypassed this comparison entirely. Knox acted
from religious conviction (salvation required literacy), Meiji Japan
from existential military threat, Korea from Cold War survival, Cuba
from revolutionary ideology. In each case, the decision was made in a
framework where education was non-negotiable, not one where it was
weighed against alternatives. No leader broke through the invisibility
by seeing education more clearly as a development input; they broke
through by needing it for a purpose that precluded comparison.

\subsection{Demographic Structure and the Fertility Transition}\label{demographic-structure-and-the-fertility-transition}

Educated women have fewer children. The decline is continuous, steepest at the bottom, and requires no threshold to trigger.

Fertility declines continuously as female secondary
completion rises. The evolutionary mechanism is a shift in reproductive
strategy, not an override of the reproductive drive: in high-mortality
environments, the optimal strategy is quantity --- have many children
because some will die. Education changes both inputs to this calculus.
Child survival rises (fewer births needed to achieve the same number of
surviving children), and the planning horizon extends --- a literate
parent can see that investing more in fewer children produces better
outcomes than hedging with numbers. The drive to maximise offspring
success is unchanged; what changes is the strategy that serves it.
The steepest fertility decline occurs at primary education
(Section~\ref{gdp-has-no-independent-effect}), where women are barely
literate. What primary education gives is capacity over preferences that
already exist --- the capacity to read a contraceptive instruction,
understand a health protocol, act on a desire not to be pregnant
continuously. Yet the demographic headwinds are also steepest here:
where total fertility rates are typically above 4, population growth is
rapid, and the gains from each educated cohort are diluted across a
larger and faster-growing next generation. As completion rises, fertility
falls, child survival improves, and household resources per child
increase. PT is a
household mechanism. States can create conditions for these decisions; they
cannot make them.

Human biology itself enforces the scale of these decisions. Lactational
amenorrhea suppresses ovulation during nursing, spacing births at
roughly two-and-a-half to three-year intervals. A woman who begins
bearing children at fifteen and has seven will still be pregnant or
nursing at thirty-five --- twenty years of continuous reproduction.
Reproduction is only the first claim on her time. The same extended
juvenile dependency that makes cultural transmission possible means that
each child requires years of feeding, supervision, and care; the last
child born at thirty-five will still need daily attention when the
mother reaches her mid-forties. This is equally true for fathers:
household structure, economic pressure, and the depth of parental
investment per child all shift categorically with fertility. High
fertility is not merely correlated with constrained lives; it is the
constraint.

Even in populations where life expectancy at birth is low, women who
survive to childbearing age typically live into their fifties or beyond;
the average is dragged down by infant and child death, not by adults
dying at forty. But survival does not mean freedom. The grandmother
hypothesis in evolutionary biology holds that human females live decades
past menopause precisely because post-reproductive women raised
grandchildren's survival odds. The long post-reproductive lifespan is
itself part of the transmission architecture. Life expectancy and
fertility are not independent metrics that happen to correlate --- they
are two readouts of the same biological architecture. The species
evolved long post-reproductive lives because it evolved extended
juvenile dependency because it evolved cultural transmission. Together
they are the biological signature of the human developmental niche.

Fertility decline is not a proxy for women's empowerment --- it is the
first expression of it. Education gives capacity over preferences that
already exist: contraceptive availability alone does not produce the
shift; education does. The further dimensions of empowerment that the
literature measures --- labour force participation, household
bargaining power, political voice, economic independence --- follow
after fertility falls, as smaller families free time, resources, and
attention. Education produces the first move; the rest cascades from it.

\subsection{Predictions}\label{predictions}

These predictions follow from the biology, not from the data --- they are stated before any empirical test is run. If the mechanism described above is correct --- if extended juvenile dependency creates a biological channel for cultural transmission, and formal education is the most powerful payload ever delivered through that channel --- then six empirical patterns must hold.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Generational timing.} Effects on development outcomes should appear at \textasciitilde25 years --- the time-to-agency interval set by juvenile dependency --- not at 10 or 15 years.\label{pred:timing}

\item \textbf{Multi-generational persistence.} Education's predictive power should decay slowly across generational depths (50, 75, 100 years), because PT transmits through the biological channel from parent to child to grandchild. Income, which has no equivalent transmission mechanism, should collapse within one generation.\label{pred:persistence}

\item \textbf{Asymmetric disruption.} Destroying the educated population should reset generational progress, because knowledge lives in the carrier, not the institution. Destroying institutions, income, or infrastructure while leaving the educated population intact should not.\label{pred:disruption}

\item \textbf{Income independence.} Because the causal path runs through education (education $\to$ cognitive capacity $\to$ household decisions $\to$ outcomes), income stripped of education's contribution should have no independent predictive power for development outcomes.\label{pred:income}

\item \textbf{Universality.} The pattern should hold across political systems, cultures, geographies, and colonial histories, because extended juvenile dependency is a species-level trait, not an institutional product.\label{pred:universality}

\item \textbf{Amplification at low baselines.} Where the state brings first-generation learners into the system, the next generation's attainment should exceed what parental transmission alone would produce ($\beta_g > 1$). At high baselines, ceiling compression reduces $\beta_g$ toward unity.\label{pred:amplification}
\end{enumerate}

Section~\ref{the-evidence} tests all six.


\section{Defining Development}\label{defining-development}

I define development as people living long lives and women having fewer children. I measure it concretely: a country is developed when it
has simultaneously achieved a \textbf{total fertility rate (TFR) below
3.65} and \textbf{life expectancy \textgreater{} 69.8 years} --- the
1960 United States values (World Bank WDI).

These two measures are chosen because they are intrinsically valuable
--- not as proxies for something else, but as the things themselves.
The UN's Human Development Index (UNDP 1990) already
treats health and education as intrinsically valuable alongside income. This definition takes that
logic to its conclusion: it retains the two that are ends in themselves
and drops the one that is not.

Life expectancy is the most fundamental measure of human welfare. Every
other outcome --- income, freedom, knowledge, dignity --- presupposes
being alive. A country where people die at 45 has failed on the most
basic term, regardless of its GDP or institutional quality. Life
expectancy is not one metric among many; it is the precondition for all
others.

The number of children a woman bears defines the shape of her entire
adult life in ways that no other variable does. Seven children means
twenty years of continuous pregnancy and nursing, followed by a further
decade of child-rearing before the last child is self-sufficient
(Section~\ref{demographic-structure-and-the-fertility-transition}).
The difference between seven children and two is not incremental; it is
the difference between a life fully absorbed by reproduction and a life
that permits education, work, civic participation, or rest.

These are also the two most direct population-level expressions of
whether the cognitive transformation produced by education has occurred.
Life expectancy tracks whether people have the planning horizon, health
knowledge, and behavioural capacity to sustain their own survival. TFR
tracks whether women have moved from biological fate to conscious
reproductive choice --- precisely the cognitive autonomy that underlies
every other dimension of human flourishing.

Every competing metric of development is either a component of life
expectancy and fertility --- child mortality is a component of LE,
nutrition is a cause of LE variation, sanitation prevents child death
that shows up in LE --- or a consequence of the cognitive
transformation that moves them (institutional quality, income,
democracy). No input can outrank the summary of itself. No consequence
can outrank its cause. LE and TFR are not the best items on a list of
development metrics --- they are what the list is measuring.

No country considered developed --- by any definition, under any
framework --- has life expectancy below 69.8 or TFR above 3.65.
Even Sen's capabilities --- health, bodily integrity, political
participation, practical reason --- all presuppose being alive and
having cognitive autonomy over one's reproductive life. Go through the
list: political participation requires forming preferences and
evaluating evidence; bodily health requires understanding what health
means and acting on it; practical reason requires the planning horizon
that education produces. Every capability on Sen's list, examined for
its generative source, has educated cognition as its enabling
precondition. On his own framework, LE and TFR are not two capabilities
among many; they are the enabling conditions for the capability to have
capabilities.

They require neither high income nor a particular type of government to
achieve. Crossing dates under three threshold specifications shift by
10--30 years but the ordering holds (Table~\ref{tab:a4}). Countries with
high expansion rates cross any threshold within 14--16 years. GDP is
excluded: measuring development through income would assume the answer.
For readers who include income in their definition: education predicts
future GDP per capita as strongly as it predicts LE and TFR
(Table~\ref{tab:2}). The definition chosen here is the conservative one
--- it strengthens the alternative's position by removing from the
outcome the one variable where income might have independent standing.

Why the 1960 USA? It is the natural baseline: 1960 marks the start of
mass decolonization, and the USA was the unambiguous global hegemon. A
country that crosses both thresholds has surpassed the richest nation on
earth on the metrics that matter for human welfare. The USA in 1960 was
mid-transition --- its TFR of 3.65 was the baby boom peak, and it did
not complete its own fertility transition until 1972. A TFR below 3.65
is itself an achievement: in 1960, only the developed world --- Europe,
Japan, and a few settler states, roughly a quarter of humanity --- had
fertility that low. The remaining three-quarters of humanity averaged a
TFR above 6 --- the human baseline.

The countries that had already crossed both thresholds by 1960 were
those with the longest prior histories of mass education: Scandinavia,
the Netherlands, France, Switzerland, the United Kingdom, Australia, and
New Zealand. Japan --- despite a TFR of 2.0 --- had life expectancy of
67.7, just below the threshold; it crossed in 1964. By 2022, 154 countries have crossed both thresholds, representing 80\%
of the world's population. Figure~\ref{fig:1} traces this accumulation over time.

\section{The Evidence}\label{the-evidence}

Education predicts every development outcome one generation forward; income, once education's contribution is removed, predicts none of them. These results are not found by searching for a pattern --- they are the six predictions of the biological mechanism (Section~\ref{predictions}), tested against 185 countries over 65 years. All six hold.

The statistical tests below formalise what the cases in Section~\ref{the-cases} demonstrate historically.

No single table in this section establishes causation on its own. The
forward lag eliminates reverse causality. The residualization
(Table~\ref{tab:3}) shows income stripped of education's contribution
predicts nothing. The shock test (Table~\ref{tab:shock}) shows that
every shock preserving the educated population preserves the fertility
transition; only destroying the educated population breaks both. The
famine test (Section~\ref{the-famine-test}) shows the converse: no
educated population has experienced famine unless external force
physically prevented food access. Each test admits an alternative story
in isolation. The causal case is that no alternative explanation
survives all of them simultaneously.

\subsection{Causal Identification}\label{causal-identification-the-bad-control-problem-and-natural-experiments}

Education predicts income one generation forward (Table~\ref{tab:2}). This is the positive finding --- and the source of the identification problem. Because education produces income, GDP sits on the causal path (education → GDP → life expectancy, fertility). Controlling for GDP strips out part of education's effect and biases the result against education (Pearl 2009; Angrist \& Pischke 2009, ch.~3). The standard approach in development economics --- regressing outcomes on income --- is therefore comparing education's product against education, with the advantage given to the product.

The identification rests on three
structures instead. First, temporal ordering: parental cohort precedes
child cohort by biological necessity, ruling out reverse causality at
the individual level. Second, the lag-decay shape (Figure~\ref{fig:3};
Section~\ref{education-predicts-development-outcomes-25-years-forward}):
education and income produce structurally different decay profiles that
autocorrelation cannot explain. Third, and most directly, a set of
natural experiments that test
Prediction~\ref{pred:disruption} --- cases where parental education was
exogenously disrupted or where other development inputs were removed
while education persisted --- produce outcomes that pure autocorrelation
cannot predict. The set includes: Cambodia (population destruction →
generational shadow); Sri Lanka (civil war, PT unbroken, development on
schedule); Myanmar (73 years of military rule, outcomes tracked
education); China (barefoot doctors dismantled, LE continued rising);
Korea vs Philippines (identical colonial base, divergent state
commitment); and the Asian Financial Crisis (income wiped, education
untouched). The cases are developed in Section~\ref{the-cases}.

\textbf{The symmetry objection.} If GDP mediates education's effect,
controlling for GDP biases against education --- but if education
partially mediates GDP's effect, controlling for education biases against
GDP. Three empirical results break the symmetry: (i) education's
predictive advantage over GDP widens to 3.3× among countries still
building their education systems
(Section~\ref{education-vs-gdp-as-predictors-of-attainment}), while
GDP's predictive power is flat across all cutoffs; (ii) education's
predictive power persists across multiple generations while income's
collapses within one (Figure~\ref{fig:3};
Section~\ref{education-predicts-development-outcomes-25-years-forward});
and (iii) natural experiments that remove income while preserving
education show development continuing on schedule
(Section~\ref{the-cases}). Autocorrelation and reverse causality cannot
produce this pattern.

\subsection{Data}\label{data}

The analysis covers 185 countries from 1950 to 2015, with education measured as the share of 20--24-year-olds who finished lower secondary school.

Education data are from WCDE v3 (Lutz et al.~2021): lower secondary
completion rates for the 20--24 age cohort, both sexes, which reflects
completed education rather than enrolment. All education percentages in
this paper refer to this measure unless otherwise noted. Coverage: 185
countries, 1950--2015 at five-year intervals.
The core generational
panel uses 1975--2015 (1,665 country-years); the residualization
analysis (Section~\ref{gdp-has-no-independent-effect}) extends to T=1960--1990 with education
interpolated to annual values for precise threshold identification.
Long-run analysis uses a 28-country subsample extending back to 1900,
selected for data availability; the generational coefficient varies
systematically with baseline education level (Section~\ref{education-vs-gdp-as-predictors-of-attainment}). GDP data:
World Bank, constant 2017 USD, per capita, log-transformed. Life
expectancy, TFR, and under-5 mortality: World Bank WDI. Parental
education: each country's lower secondary completion lagged 25 years
(T−25).

\subsection{Completion as the Operative Variable}\label{completion-as-the-operative-variable}

This paper measures whether people finished school, not how well they
scored on tests. Completion is the right measure for a generational
mechanism.

Formal education --- even at the lowest quality --- represents a
categorical discontinuity from oral cultural transmission. The
neuroscience is unambiguous: literacy physically restructures the brain's
visual processing pathways (Dehaene et al.~2010), and numeracy recruits
parietal circuits unavailable to innumerate populations (Gordon 2004;
Frank et al.~2008). These are not marginal improvements on existing
capacities --- they are new cognitive technologies ---
\emph{categorical literacy} --- that do not develop without schooling,
regardless of intelligence or environment.

\paragraph{Why exposure, not learning, is the operative variable.}

The first-generation learner undergoes a categorical change by being in
school --- not by scoring well on tests. What formal schooling provides
is not a specific curriculum but sustained exposure to literate culture:
structured time, abstract categorisation, written language, numeracy,
adult authority organised around knowledge transmission. The longer the
child remains inside this environment, the deeper the cognitive
reorganisation. Primary education gives capacity over reproduction ---
the ability to read a contraceptive instruction, understand a health
protocol, act on desires that already exist. Secondary gives capacity
over health navigation, economic participation, civic engagement.
Tertiary gives capacity over complex systems. Each level is not a
separate input; it is more time inside the categorical change. The dose
is duration of exposure, not fidelity of instruction.

This is why completion predicts development outcomes and test scores do
not. Hanushek \& Woessmann (2008, 2015) measure what the child learned
--- the payload's fidelity. The mechanism operates on the exposure
itself. A child who completed nine years of poor-quality schooling and
scored badly on every assessment has still spent nine years inside an
environment that reorganised their cognition categorically relative to a
parent who never entered the building. The test score measures how well
the payload was delivered. Completion measures whether the child was
inside the channel long enough for the reorganisation to consolidate.
Development outcomes track the second, not the first.

\paragraph{Why content does not matter.}

Tests measure breadth of content retention at a point in time. Content is
the dimension that disappears. A software engineer does not remember
10th-grade biology. A doctor does not remember trigonometry. Decades
later, the specific material is gone. If the mechanism operated through
content --- through what the student learned --- then education would
depreciate as content is forgotten. It does not. No generation in any
country has completed school at a lower rate than its parents, and the
life-course returns to education show no decay with age. The gains
persist because they do not run through content. They run through
something that content exposure produced but that survives the content's
disappearance.

What survives is the cumulative cognitive reorganisation. Years of
sustained exposure to biology, chemistry, history, mathematics, and
literature do not produce adults who remember all five. They produce
adults who spent years inside five different formal knowledge systems ---
each with its own mode of abstraction, its own vocabulary, its own
relationship between evidence and conclusion. The breadth of the
curriculum is not an attempt to produce polymaths. It is sustained
duration of exposure across multiple modes of formal reasoning, each
contributing to the same cumulative reorganisation. Tests measure whether
the student retained the content of each subject. The mechanism does not
operate through retention. It operates through the years the student
spent inside the reorganising environment. The content is the vehicle;
the reorganisation is the payload. The vehicle is disposable. The payload
is permanent.

This connects the non-depreciation result to the completion-vs-quality
debate. Tests predict that education should depreciate --- content is
forgotten, scores would decline if re-tested, specific knowledge fades.
Education does not depreciate. Therefore whatever persists is not what
tests measure. Completion captures it. Test scores do not.

\paragraph{The generational gate.}

The distinction sharpens at the generational gate. The educated parent's
role in PT is not to teach their child what they learned in school. It is
to hold the expectation that their child belongs in school --- to see
completion as normal, to monitor attendance, to assume that the educated
world is where their child will live. This expectation is the gate. An
uneducated parent cannot open it, because they have no experience of the
world on the other side. An educated parent opens it automatically ---
through self-interest, not ideology --- because school is where they came
from and where their child belongs. The gate does not require the parent
to be a good teacher. It requires the parent to have been a student.

This is also why the grandparent effect is strongest at low baselines.
Where education is scarce, the grandparent is a second educated person
in the household --- and, in a village where education is rare, a visible
exemplar to the wider community. At high baselines, the entire society
holds this expectation and the grandparent's independent contribution
disappears --- exactly the pattern the data show (grandparent $\beta$
significant below 50\% parental completion, zero above it).

\paragraph{From reorganisation to specialisation.}

The reorganised individual naturally specialises. Categorical literacy
--- the capacity for systematic learning, abstract reasoning, structured
problem-solving --- is not an end state. It is the precondition for
further skill acquisition. The educated individual can absorb trade
skills through apprenticeship, on-the-job training through employment,
formal knowledge through college, deep expertise through a PhD. The
specific pathway does not matter. What matters is that each pathway
requires the cognitive infrastructure that formal schooling built. The
uneducated individual is not excluded from specialisation by lack of
opportunity alone. They are excluded because the capacity to learn a
structured skill systematically --- to read a manual, follow a protocol,
internalise an abstract procedure --- was never constructed.

Specialisation produces the division of labour. The division of labour
produces productivity. Productivity is wealth. This is the causal
chain that Adam Smith observed at its terminal node. He saw educated
workers dividing their labour with extraordinary productivity and built a
theory of wealth from what he saw. He never asked where the educated
workers came from. Economics has taken educated labour as given ever
since.

This completes the chain that the residualisation result
(Table~\ref{tab:3}) measures statistically. Income is the end of the
sequence: education $\to$ cognitive reorganisation $\to$ specialisation
capacity $\to$ division of labour $\to$ productivity $\to$ wealth.
Stripping education's contribution from income and finding that the
remainder predicts nothing is not a statistical anomaly. It is the
arithmetic consequence of income being the last link in a chain whose
first link is education. The relationship between income and life
expectancy that has anchored development economics since 1975 was never
income purchasing health. It was education producing both income and
health through the same cognitive reorganisation, with income arriving as
a visible by-product and receiving credit for outcomes it did not cause.

\paragraph{The empirical confirmation.}

A direct test confirms the mechanistic argument. On the 85 countries
where both completion rates and harmonised test scores exist (Angrist et
al.~2021), I run a horse race: which measure better predicts life
expectancy, TFR, and child mortality? With country fixed effects at a
10-year forward lag, completion explains 26\% of within-country TFR
variation; test scores explain 0.5\% ($p = 0.48$). For child mortality
the ratio is similar: completion $R^2 = 0.48$, test scores $R^2 = 0.03$
($p = 0.24$). Hanushek \& Woessmann's quality measure predicts GDP
growth --- their dependent variable. It does not predict development
outcomes.

The quality debate in development economics --- whether learning outcomes
or years of schooling matter more --- is measuring the wrong dimension of
the right variable. Quality matters for GDP growth, which is Hanushek \&
Woessmann's dependent variable. It does not matter for the fertility
transition, child survival, life expectancy, or intergenerational
transmission --- the outcomes that constitute development. The empirical
result (completion $R^2 = 0.26$ for TFR vs.\ test scores
$R^2 = 0.005$) is not a puzzle. It is the mechanism's prediction:
exposure is what reorganises cognition and opens the generational gate;
test performance is what determines how much income the reorganised
individual earns. Both are real. Only one is the cause of development.

\subsection{Empirical Strategy}\label{empirical-strategy}

Each country is compared only to its own past. The question: does parental education predict what happens to their children one generation later?

Primary specification --- country fixed effects regression:

\[E_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta_1 E_{i,t-25} + \varepsilon_{it}\]

In plain language: a country's education level today is predicted by its own education level 25 years ago (the parents' generation). The $\alpha_i$ term gives each country its own baseline --- so the test is not ``which countries are more educated?'' but ``within the same country, does the previous generation's education predict the next generation's?'' This absorbs everything permanent about a country: institutions, geography, culture, colonial history. What remains is change over time. The 25-year lag is not a researcher choice --- it is the time-to-agency interval derived from the biological mechanism (Prediction~\ref{pred:timing}).

Comparison specifications replace parental education with income, or include both:

\[E_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta_2 \ln Y_{it} + \varepsilon_{it}\]

\[E_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta_1 E_{i,t-25} + \beta_2 \ln Y_{it} + \varepsilon_{it}\]

For development outcomes (life expectancy, fertility, child mortality), I measure outcomes 25 years after the education measurement to eliminate reverse causality --- education at time T predicting life expectancy at time T+25:

\[O_{i,t+25} = \alpha_i + \gamma_1 E_{it} + \gamma_2 O_{it} + \varepsilon_{it}\]

Including the initial outcome level ($O_{it}$) is deliberately conservative: it absorbs the country's prior trajectory, so the test asks whether education predicts improvement beyond whatever trend was already underway.\footnote{The main analysis uses aggregate (both-sexes) lower secondary completion. Female completion produces stronger R² on every outcome tested (Section~\ref{gdp-has-no-independent-effect}); the aggregate is the more conservative specification, and the results reported here are a lower bound.}

\textbf{Residualization strategy.} If education causes income, then part of
income's apparent predictive power is really education's effect in disguise.
To test whether income contributes \emph{anything on its own}, I strip out
the portion of income that education produced and use only the remainder
to predict development outcomes 25 years forward. This is a standard
decomposition (Frisch-Waugh-Lovell), applied across three education levels
(primary, lower secondary, upper secondary), four outcomes (life
expectancy, TFR, child mortality, child education), and multiple sample
definitions.\footnote{See \texttt{scripts/edu\_vs\_gdp\_residualized.py},
\texttt{scripts/edu\_vs\_gdp\_tfr\_residualized.py},
\texttt{scripts/edu\_vs\_gdp\_child\_edu\_residualized.py}. An entry-cohort
design supplements the cutoff analysis: for each threshold (10\% to 90\%),
identify the first year each country crosses it, then include all
observations from that point forward while education remains below a
ceiling (50\%--90\%). See \texttt{scripts/edu\_vs\_gdp\_entry\_ceiling.py}.}

\textbf{On year fixed effects.} Adding year FE collapses the parental
coefficient to $\beta$=0.083 (p=0.07) on the full panel --- a known
pathology of staggered adoption that Appendix~\ref{appendix-robustness}
addresses with a Goodman-Bacon decomposition and a Callaway--Sant'Anna
heterogeneity-robust estimator.

completion below 30\%, where most of the remaining 20\% of humanity sits

ranging from 2.0× to 3.3×.\footnote{See

their parents' education level, $\beta_g$ would be 1 or less. In fact it
exceeds 1: at the \textless{}20\% cutoff, $\beta_g$=1.032 (t=5.8);
at \textless{}10\%, $\beta_g$=1.019 (Table~\ref{tab:a1}). The state adds reach
on top of what households transmit.

The amplification is strongest where baselines are lowest: among
countries below 20\% completion, the next generation's gain is 2.86×
the parents' level (education explains 70\% of the variation); below
50\%, 1.83× (explains 79\%); below 90\%, 1.24× (explains

not weaker, than the post-1975 panel.

(Section~\ref{the-decision}) shows a flatter curve: $\beta_g$=4.4 at 1.5\% declining
more slowly to 0.4 at 49\%. The S-curve structure is robust across all
countries with sufficient historical depth. Bangladesh shows
$\beta_g$≈2 throughout its observed trajectory (1--18\% baseline),
consistent with a country still in the steep part of the S-curve. India
follows a similar pattern at $\beta_g$≈1.6 through 28\% baseline.

\subsubsection*{Lag-decay shape (Prediction~\ref{pred:persistence})}

A great-grandparent's education still predicts how long their great-grandchild will live; a great-grandparent's income does not.

The test applied to every competing account in this paper is whether it
survives at generational depth. PT's signature is multi-generational
durability: education at time $T$ predicts outcomes at $T{+}25$,
$T{+}50$, $T{+}75$, and $T{+}100$ --- four generations. Any variable
that collapses within one generation is by that fact downstream rather
than causal. Income cannot be tested beyond 25 years: pre-literate subsistence
societies had uniformly low income (\textasciitilde\$400--600 per capita,
constant 2017 USD), not missing data --- and the uniformity is itself
evidence, because income variation requires the cognitive capacity that
education produces. Institutions, quality scores, and provision all fail
the same test: none survives at the depth that education reaches. This is not a
separate finding for each alternative --- it is a single methodological
principle derived from the mechanism itself.

Figure~\ref{fig:3} confirms the predicted asymmetry across lag lengths. Education's
predictive power for life expectancy decays slowly across three
generational depths: R²=0.562 at lag 0, R²=0.364
at lag 25, R²=0.171 at lag 50, R²=0.085 at lag 75. Income's collapses
within one generation --- already fading by lag 20--25. Backfilling pre-1960 income at \$500
does not rescue income's predictive power.\footnote{See
\texttt{scripts/backfill\_income\_lag\_test.py}.}

\subsubsection*{The grandparent channel}\label{the-grandparent-channel}

The lag-decay results show that education predicts across multiple
generations. A sharper test: does the grandparent's education predict
outcomes \emph{independently}, after controlling for the parent's own
education?

For fertility in the full panel, the answer is no ---
grandparent education adds nothing once the parent's is
controlled ($\beta_{gp}$=+0.001, $p$=0.80). The parent absorbs the
grandparent's entire contribution to fertility. But restrict to
countries where education is still scarce (parent completion
below 50\%) and the grandparent effect emerges: $\beta_{gp}$=$-$0.059
($p$=0.0003), nearly twice the parent's own coefficient
($\beta_p$=$-$0.033). R² jumps from 0.39 to 0.46. Where education is
scarce, having an educated grandparent independently reduces fertility
beyond what the parent's own education predicts. The effect is not
gendered: grandfather and grandmother education produce comparable
coefficients in the low-education subsample ($\beta$=$-$0.054 and
$-$0.050 respectively), consistent with oblique transmission --- in a
village where education is rare, any educated elder is a visible exemplar.

For life expectancy, the grandparent effect is significant across all
education levels ($\beta_{gp}$=+0.070, $p$\textless0.0001), adding 3.6
percentage points of R² beyond the parent alone. Roughly half the
grandparent's total effect on LE runs through the parent; half is
direct. For grandchild education, the effect is strongest:
$\beta_{gp}$=+0.271 ($p$\textless0.0001), R² gain of 5.2 points.

The pattern is what the generational gate predicts
(Section~\ref{completion-as-the-operative-variable}): at low baselines
each educated person in the household adds independent weight; at high
baselines the parent has internalised everything the grandparent could
offer. The grandparent effect on fertility is real, but it operates
precisely where it matters most --- the steepest part of the
transition.\footnote{See
\texttt{scripts/robustness/grandparent\_effect.py}.}

This finding generates a specific prediction no alternative framework
produces: the grandparent's \emph{presence} should matter, not just
their education level. If the channel is household-level transmission
--- an educated body in the home modelling behaviour and reinforcing
norms --- then a living educated grandparent in the household should
improve grandchild outcomes beyond what the parent's education alone
explains. A deceased grandparent of equal education should not. DHS
co-residence data can test this directly: compare grandchild fertility
and educational attainment in households where an educated grandparent
is present versus households where they are absent, controlling for
parent education. If the effect is real, it confirms that PT operates
through physical presence and daily interaction, not merely through
inherited socioeconomic status.

that had, by 2001, already crossed both development thresholds.

channel: that local income proxies for child survival only after 2000
and only where household education already exists to convert provision
into behaviour is not income purchasing health --- it is education's
product, arriving from outside. The channel is
fragile by construction: it runs on donor altruism, not self-interest,
and development assistance for health is already
declining.\footnote{See \texttt{scripts/u5mr\_by\_edu\_level.py}.}

\subsubsection*{Composition by education level}

Primary education drives fertility decline; deeper education drives life expectancy gains.

The \emph{composition} of the
effect varies by level. Primary education is
the strongest predictor of fertility decline (R²=0.65 vs 0.48 for lower
secondary, 0.32 for upper secondary): the first educated generation ---
the categorical jump from no schooling to literacy --- is where
fertility falls hardest, universally, across every country in the
dataset regardless of culture, religion, or economic structure.
Caldwell (1976) predicts fertility decline when children shift from economic
assets to costs; if correct, the steepest decline should track economic
modernisation, not primary education. It tracks primary education, in
agrarian and industrial contexts alike. The mechanism is not economic
calculation; it is cognitive capacity.
Life expectancy gains accumulate
with deeper education: upper secondary (R²=0.48) predicts better than
primary (R²=0.41). This is the structure that Lutz \& Kebede
(2018) identified when redrawing the income--mortality curve with education on the
x-axis: the fertility transition is triggered by the first level, while
the education--health relationship tightens at higher levels.

Residualized GDP R² never exceeds 0.03 for life
expectancy and is zero for fertility, at every level.

The education--life expectancy relationship shows no diminishing returns ---
linear from primary through
tertiary (Lutz \& Kebede 2018). Among 74 countries with lower-secondary
completion above 85\% in 2010, college completion carries a within-group
correlation of r=0.45 with life expectancy --- a 5.7-year gradient
between lowest and highest college-completion quartiles among countries
that have already solved the lower-secondary problem.\footnote{See
\texttt{scripts/verify\_college\_le\_gradient.py}.}

\subsubsection*{Robustness and additional tests}

Every robustness test confirms the residualization result (Table~\ref{tab:3}).
Residualized GDP R² stays below 0.003 for life expectancy and fertility
across all lag lengths tested; under-5 mortality reaches 0.023
(Table~\ref{tab:a2}). The confidence intervals do not overlap: education R²
[0.33, 0.59] vs residualized GDP R² [0.00, 0.04] (500-replication
bootstrap). An alternative estimation method designed
specifically to correct for bias in short panels (Anderson-Hsiao
instrumental variables) produces results within 3--10\% of the main
estimates.\footnote{See \texttt{scripts/robustness\_tests.py}.} Results are unchanged when time-varying institutional quality scores (Polity5 polity2) are added (Appendix~\ref{the-institutional-challenge}). Female
education produces stronger results than the aggregate on every outcome.

The parental income test asks a simple question: does family income
predict child education independently, or only because richer parents
tend to be more educated? Parental income alone predicts child education
moderately ($\beta$=15.4, R²=0.293). But add parental education to the
same regression and income's coefficient collapses by 72\% to $\beta$=4.3
(p=0.04), adding almost nothing (R²=0.014) beyond education alone.
Education's coefficient barely moves ($\beta$=0.475 from 0.553) while
income's collapses. Income transmits through education, not vice versa.

The Asian Financial Crisis (1997--98) tests Prediction~\ref{pred:disruption}: GDP collapsed across five countries (Indonesia −14.5\%,
Thailand −8.8\%, Malaysia −9.6\%, Philippines −3.0\%, Korea −6\%).
Lower secondary completion continued uninterrupted in all five;
Indonesia gained 5.4pp while losing 14.5\% of GDP. Income was destroyed; the educated population was not; development continued (see
\texttt{scripts/asian\_financial\_crisis.py}).

Once education is included, initial fertility barely predicts
subsequent fertility one generation forward (Table~\ref{tab:2}, Panel A, row 3: initial outcome β=0.039).
Demographic momentum does not drive the transition --- educated women's decisions do.

TFR is a household decision running on PT --- internal to the household,

\textbf{Russia} (99\% lower secondary completion, 1990). The Soviet
dissolution crashed life expectancy from 69.5 to 64.5 (1988--1994) ---
5 years lost in six. TFR, already at 1.89, continued below replacement
throughout --- 1.20 by 2000, never reversing. The state dissolved; the
educated population did not. LE returned to its pre-collapse level by
2009 (68.7) and reached 73.1 by 2019 --- surpassing the Soviet-era peak

to state collapse.\footnote{See

\textbf{South Africa and the HIV epidemic.} South Africa's 65\% lower
secondary completion in 1990 is an aggregate masking apartheid
stratification: near-universal white completion and much lower Black
completion produced a bimodal distribution invisible in national data.
The population bearing the brunt of HIV had far lower education than the
headline figure suggests. HIV crashed LE from 62.9 to 53.9
(1990--2005) --- the largest peacetime LE reversal in modern history.
TFR fell from 3.72 to 2.41 over the same period, uninterrupted.

The Southern African epidemic was driven primarily by factors education
could not have prevented: low traditional circumcision prevalence (8--11\%
in the HIV belt vs 85--99\% in Muslim West Africa), which three
randomised trials showed provides a 60\% biological reduction in male
acquisition risk (Auvert et al.~2005); and circular mining migration
that created connected sexual networks across the subcontinent. These
are genuinely exogenous to education.

But education was in the story from the beginning --- on both sides.
Early in the epidemic, more educated Africans were \emph{more} likely to
be HIV-positive: greater mobility, more partners, more agency exercised
without information about a novel pathogen (Fortson 2008). Once
information existed, the gradient reversed: educated populations changed
behaviour first. In Botswana, a natural experiment exploiting a 1996
school reform showed that each additional year of secondary schooling
\emph{caused} an 8.1-percentage-point reduction in cumulative HIV risk
(De~Neve et al.~2015). In rural Uganda, educated individuals responded
more to prevention campaigns, and the education--HIV gradient reversed
from positive to negative between 1990 and 2000 (De~Walque 2007). Baker
et al.\ (2017) formalise this as the Population Education Transition
curve: when a novel health risk enters a population, the educated are
early adopters (positive gradient); once information exists, the
educated change behaviour first (gradient reverses). The pattern
replicates across cigarettes, processed food, and HIV --- education is
the mechanism in both directions.

The fertility transition confirms the household channel. TFR runs on PT
--- internal to the household, unreachable by a pathogen that operates
through sexual networks. LE depends on whether external conditions allow
survival. Post-ARV, LE recovered to 66.1 by 2019. The TFR--LE divergence during the HIV crisis is itself
diagnostic: the household mechanism continued; the external shock could
not reach it.\footnote{See
\texttt{scripts/verify\_russia\_south\_africa.py}.}

Sri Lanka (Section~\ref{sri-lanka}) and the Asian Financial Crisis
(Section~\ref{gdp-has-no-independent-effect}) confirm the pattern from
different angles: civil war delayed LE without breaking the fertility
trajectory; income destruction left both outcomes untouched.

Cambodia (Section~\ref{cambodia-the-pt-shadow}) is the exception that
proves the rule: the only shock that breaks the household mechanism is
the one that destroys the household.

\subsection{The Famine Test}\label{the-famine-test}

The shock test asks whether education survives crisis. The famine test asks the converse: does education \emph{prevent} the most extreme form of crisis?

Every major famine since 1950 occurred in a low-education setting. Of 21 famines in the dataset, 19 occurred where lower secondary completion was below 50\%. The median education level at the time of famine was 19.6\%; the mean was 25.4\%. The two exceptions --- North Korea (1996, 100\% completion) and Yemen (2018, 68\%) --- both required external force physically preventing food access: totalitarian state control of distribution in the first case, naval blockade in the second. Where the educated population retained agency, famine did not occur.

The near-miss cases make the pattern sharper. Seventeen countries faced shocks comparable to or worse than those that produced famines elsewhere --- Cuba's 35\% GDP collapse when Soviet food imports were cut by 80\% (1993), post-WWII Japan and Germany, Armenia under simultaneous blockade and war (1993), Ukraine under Russian invasion and grain blockade (2022) --- and none experienced famine. Their median education was 71.6\%, against 19.6\% for famine countries. The difference is significant at $p < 0.00001$ (Mann--Whitney $U$).

Educated populations do not starve. They reorganise supply chains, ration collectively, substitute crops, demand state response, and migrate strategically. Uneducated populations, facing the same shock, lack the household-level capacity to adapt and the political capacity to compel response. The mechanism is PT operating through the household: the same cognitive and behavioural repertoire that drives the fertility transition also drives famine prevention.

\textbf{Bihar and Kerala, 1966.} The within-country case eliminates every confounder except education. The 1965--66 national drought crashed India's grain production by 19\%. Bihar experienced famine: 70,000--130,000 excess deaths (Dyson \& Maharatna 1992). Kerala --- India's most food-deficit state, 40\% import-dependent, hit so severely that food riots erupted and the UK Parliament identified it as one of the ``most severely hit areas'' --- had no famine. Same Constitution, same free press, same central government, same democratic institutions. Bihar's literacy was 22\% (female: 9\%). Kerala's was 55\% (female: 39\%).

Sen's claim that ``no famine has ever taken place in a functioning democracy'' (Sen 1999) cannot explain the Bihar--Kerala divergence. Democracy was constant across both states. Education was not. Myhrvold-Hanssen (2003) challenges Sen directly on this point, arguing that Bihar 1966--67 meets any reasonable definition of famine and that the Indian media, ``although free and independent, did not provide reliable information.'' Drèze himself described the evidence that Bihar was a success story as ``precious little'' (Brass 1986).

Kerala's own history confirms the mechanism. In 1943, Travancore (pre-independence Kerala) experienced famine under the same structural vulnerability --- extreme food-import dependence --- killing approximately 90,000 people. The difference between 1943 and 1966 was not democracy (Travancore had an elected legislature from 1932) but education: literacy had risen from approximately 30\% to 55\%. The educated population built the institutions --- universal public distribution, food committees, political mobilisation --- that prevented the same structural vulnerability from producing the same outcome.\footnote{See \texttt{scripts/famine\_education\_test.py}.}

The earliest modern example is Japan (1872): the Meiji compulsory

the inputs.

\subsection{Taiwan and Korea}\label{taiwan-and-korea}

Taiwan and Korea crossed earliest --- both at benchmark pace,
both state-driven from colonial bases built by Japan (25\% and 18\%
completion by 1950). Korea expanded at 2.13 pp/yr, the fastest sustained
rate in the WCDE dataset; Taiwan followed a nearly identical trajectory.
Both had functioning markets --- what Sen calls ``growth-mediated
security'' --- but the growth was mediated by education, not the reverse.
State-driven singular expansion compressed development to 20--34 years
against the 40--70 years seen in competing-priority cases.

American colonial education in the
Philippines was similarly deep --- 22\% lower secondary completion by
1950, comparable to Korea. In 1960, the Philippines was one of the
most prosperous countries in developing Asia: GDP per capita of \$1,124
against Korea's \$1,038 (constant 2017 USD), ahead of Thailand (\$592),
Indonesia (\$598), and far ahead of India (\$313) and China (\$241). The
Philippines had both the educational base and the income advantage. But
no post-independence government sustained the educational investment.
Korea made education the singular priority and expanded at 2.13 pp/yr;
the Philippines drifted at roughly half that pace. The result is visible
in Figure~\ref{fig:2}: comparable colonial base, comparable income, divergent
post-colonial trajectory. Education policy is a choice, not an inheritance. Income does not make the choice for you.

\subsection{Kerala}\label{kerala}

Kerala crossed at \textasciitilde1982 --- the paradigm case of
competing-priority expansion. Education had been building since the
early twentieth century through social reform movements --- gradual
accumulation through social reform and state investment ---
competing-priority, not singular-priority at Korea pace. TFR
crossed by \textasciitilde1974; LE followed \textasciitilde8 years
later. Kerala had extensive state provision --- public health, food
distribution, land reform --- but fertility declined because educated
women made the decision, not because provision was available
(Section~\ref{demographic-structure-and-the-fertility-transition}). The
contrast within India confirms this: during the Emergency (1975--77),
forced sterilisation campaigns failed in the least-educated northern
states and brought down the government (Gwatkin 1979; Vicziany 1982).
Kerala needed no coercion. State coercion either arrives after education
has done the work, or fails where education has not.

The sub-national test has been run systematically. Drèze \& Murthi
(2001), using district-level panel data for 1981 and 1991, found that
women's education was the single most important predictor of fertility
differences across Indian districts, while urbanisation, poverty
reduction, and male literacy showed no significant association. The
cross-country result replicates within a single country at district
level.

\subsection{China}\label{china}

China crossed in 1994. Three corrections to the standard narrative.

First, what is called an educational catastrophe was
the largest educational expansion in Chinese history. The standard
framing conflates the disruption of university education --- a
small percentage of the population --- with the overall
trajectory. For rural China --- 80\% of the population
(National Bureau of Statistics 1982) --- the Cultural Revolution era
produced the largest lower secondary gains in the WCDE
1870--2015 record: +10.6pp for the 1975 cohort, +15.0pp for the 1980
cohort. Community schools (民办学校) brought secondary
education to villages that had none (Pepper 1996; Unger 1982; Gao 2008).

\textbf{Second: the provision debate is misconceived.} Drèze \& Sen
(1989, Table~10.6) show China's under-5 mortality at 32\% of what GNP
predicted --- the largest deviation in their sample --- and attribute it
to ``support-led security'': barefoot doctors (赤脚医生), direct
provision. But Sen regressed on income and found a residual. That
residual is education.

When life expectancy and under-5 mortality are matched on mean years of
schooling (age 20--24, $\pm$0.5~years) rather than GNP, China's
advantage reverses. China's LE was \emph{below} education-matched peers
from 1965 to 1991 --- 6.3~years below at 1965, still 2.4 below at 1980,
converging only in 1991 --- and its U5MR was \emph{above}
education-matched peers until 2000.\footnote{See
\texttt{scripts/china\_mean\_yrs\_vs\_peers.py}. Mean years of schooling
computed from WCDE~v3 proportions (age 20--24, both sexes); LE and U5MR
from World Bank WDI.} The barefoot doctors were not producing
exceptional outcomes; China was underperforming what its education level
predicted.

The barefoot doctors and the community school teachers came from the same
CR-era rural mobilisation (Gao 1999). The doctors were local, culturally embedded,
drawn from the villages they served --- optimally delivered provision.
When Deng dismantled the system after 1980, they left, because rational
people freed from revolutionary ideology redirected their labour toward
self-interest. The LE convergence rate was statistically unchanged:
$+0.28$~years/year before 1981, $+0.29$ after ($\beta_3 = +0.007$, $p =
0.78$). LE continued to rise --- from 64 to 69.8 by 1994 --- because
the educational baseline built into rural households did not leave with
the doctors. Drèze \& Sen (1989, pp.~215--221) document the resulting
health crises and rising rural medical costs (Gao 2008; Liu et
al.~2003), but these crises left no detectable mark on the trajectory.

China crossed to \emph{above} education-predicted LE in 1992 and to
\emph{below} education-predicted U5MR around 2000 --- two decades after
the barefoot doctors were gone. Mean years of schooling rose from 5.9
(1965) to 8.0 (1980) to 9.6 (2000), tracking both outcomes across both
eras.

Third, the TFR threshold was crossed in
1975 --- five years before the one-child policy. China's
fertility trajectory mirrors South Korea and Thailand's, both of
which achieved equivalent TFRs without compulsory policy (Cai 2010; Miller et
al.~2018).

\subsection{Cambodia: The PT Shadow}\label{cambodia-the-pt-shadow}

Cambodia is the direct test of Prediction~\ref{pred:disruption}: what happens when the educated population is destroyed. The country experienced severe disruption under the Khmer
Rouge (1975--1979), during which the education system collapsed. The
data shows two distinct stalls. The first is the exogenous shock itself.
The second --- a generation later --- is PT's prediction: the
household baseline is the binding constraint (Section~\ref{the-generational-transmission-mechanism}, PT mechanism).

\textbf{First stall (1975--1985).} Completion was 10.1\% in 1975, fell to 9.4\% by 1980, and was still 9.5\% in 1985 --- a decade of zero progress.

\textbf{Recovery and second stall (1990--2010).} After the Paris Accords
and UN transitional authority (1991--1993), schools were rebuilt with
sustained international investment. Completion jumped to 35.1\% by 1995
on the back of that reconstruction. Then it stalled again. From 1995 to
2010, completion plateaued at 31--36\%, unmoved despite continued
external funding. The buildings were there; the teachers were there; the
money was there. Progress did not come. The 35\% plateau is what
competing-priority expansion looks like when it interacts with a
parental education shadow: international reconstruction dispersed
resources across health, infrastructure, governance, and education
simultaneously, while the parental cohort remained frozen at
\textasciitilde10\%. Korea proves singular priority can push far beyond
any parental baseline --- but Cambodia after 1991 was not Korea. The
plateau reflects dispersed investment meeting a damaged generational
base.

The first stall is the exogenous shock --- the regime destroyed the
education system. PT predicts the second. It is the generational shadow:
the children reaching secondary school in 1996--2010 were born
approximately 1982--1996 --- their parents were the cohort whose own
education was frozen at \textasciitilde10\% during 1975--1985 --- the
pre-disruption level, preserved in households even as the school system
collapsed. Countries that started at Cambodia's level in 1960 reached a
median of 21\% by 1985. The children of the frozen cohort inherited a
10\% parental baseline instead of a \textasciitilde21\% one; the plateau
reflects that missing growth. Recovery
from 2011 onward corresponds to the post-disruption cohort's children
finally dominating the school-age population. The regime fell in 1979,
but education remained frozen through 1985 (9.5\%). Twenty-five years
from the end of the educational disruption (1985) lands at 2010 ---
precisely when recovery began.

Countries that started at Cambodia's level in 1960 reached a median of
46\% by 2015. Cambodia reached 36\%. The buildings came back in 1991.
Progress came back in 2011. That twenty-year gap is the PT shadow.

The shadow is not over. The grandparent channel
(Section~\ref{the-grandparent-channel}) predicts a second, deeper lag.
Children reaching adulthood in 2020--2025 have parents from the
post-reconstruction cohort (35--36\% completion) but grandparents from
the frozen cohort (9--10\%). At low baselines, grandparent education
predicts child outcomes independently of parent education, with a
coefficient nearly twice the parent's
($\beta_{gp}$\,=\,--0.059 vs $\beta_p$\,=\,--0.033 for fertility).
The Khmer Rouge fell in 1979; the parental shadow lifted in 2011;
the grandparent shadow persists through at least 2035, when the first
post-disruption cohort's children become grandparents themselves.


\section{The Decision}\label{the-decision}

Every country that developed made the same decision: sustained investment in education. The only variation is speed.

Spain is the longest counter-case: one of the wealthiest empires in
history, it had 0.3\% lower secondary completion for the 1900 birth
cohort and did not reach universal completion until approximately 2000.
Wealth was present for 450 years. Mass education took that long to
arrive. Korea compressed the same transition to one generation. The
mechanism is the same; what varies is how fast the state acts.

The state is necessary for scale but not for efficacy. PT operates at
the household level regardless of state form --- autocracy, democracy,
colonial administration, revolutionary government. What the state
provides is reach: the extension of education to children whose parents
have none. The state is the delivery mechanism, not the cause. The era
of no priority --- the pre-industrial baseline where hierarchy
suppressed mass education --- is over. The global education signal is
now visible everywhere: every government on earth claims to prioritise
education. The question is no longer whether to educate but at what
speed. Competing priority is the norm. The decision this paper calls for
is singular priority.

The question for the remaining 20\% is whether evidence can now
substitute for the contingent motivations that drove every previous
breakthrough. Knox, Meiji, and Korea bypassed the cost-benefit frame by
never entering it; the commitment preceded any calculation.
Evidence-based commitment would bypass the frame from the other
direction: by showing that the calculation has already been done across
185 countries and the answer is always the same --- education first,
everything else follows.

The function of the evidence is not to win a cost-benefit comparison
against health or nutrition, which education will always lose at
decision-relevant timescales, but to remove the comparison altogether:
to establish that education is not one investment among many but the
precondition for all others.

This framework makes a falsifiable prediction. Uganda reached 48.8\%
lower secondary completion in 2025, expanding at approximately 6
percentage points per five-year period. Its TFR is 4.39 and life
expectancy 67.7 --- below both development thresholds. If the framework
is correct, Uganda will cross both thresholds (TFR\,<\,3.65 and
LE\,>\,69.8) by approximately 2050, conditional on maintaining current
expansion rates. The 25-year forward lag from 2025 education levels
predicts outcomes in 2050; the grandparent channel
(Section~\ref{the-grandparent-channel}) predicts the crossing will be
modestly delayed by Uganda's low grandparent baseline (9.1\% in 1975
for the 2025 cohort). If Uganda crosses on schedule, the framework is
confirmed in a case it did not train on. If it does not, and expansion
was sustained, the framework is wrong.

No leader has yet made the decision on the
basis of evidence alone. This paper provides the evidence. The decision
remains.

\textbf{Why Model 4 shows $\beta$=0.083 and why it does not matter.}
When each country is compared only to its own past (one-way fixed
effects), a 10-point increase in parental education predicts a 4.8-point
increase in child education one generation later ($\beta$=0.483). Adding
year fixed effects --- which also control for which \emph{decade} an
observation falls in --- collapses this to $\beta$=0.083. The question
is whether this collapse reflects a real weakness in the evidence or a
flaw in the statistical method.

Recent econometrics has shown it is the method. Goodman-Bacon (2021)
proved that when you add year controls to a panel where countries adopted
education at different times, the result is a weighted average of every
possible before-and-after comparison between pairs of country groups.
Some of these comparisons are clean: countries that expanded education
compared against countries that had not yet started. Others are
contaminated: countries just beginning their expansion compared against
countries already in the middle of theirs --- as if measuring whether a
runner accelerated by comparing her to someone already
sprinting.\footnote{A formal decomposition of the education panel
confirms this: the clean comparisons show large effects; the contaminated
ones show near-zero effects; and the contaminated comparisons dominate
the average. See
\texttt{scripts/robustness/goodman\_bacon\_decomposition.py}.}

Models 1--3 partly address this by restricting to countries still
expanding education, where the effect is strong. The
Callaway--Sant'Anna (2021) estimator addresses it directly: it compares
each country only against countries that have not yet started expanding,
eliminating the contaminated pairings altogether. The result: crossing
the 10\% education threshold produces a gain of 7.9 percentage points in
child education one generation later (95\% CI: 4.4--11.0).

The event study (Figure~\ref{fig:cs-event}) makes the pattern visible.
Before a country crosses the 10\% threshold, there is no effect ---
countries that will expand and countries that will not are on identical
trajectories. After crossing, the effect grows steadily: +1.3 percentage
points immediately, +6.6 at ten years, +14.9 at twenty-five years,
+21.4 at thirty-five years. Parents who cross the education threshold
raise more educated children, who raise still more educated children.
The effect compounds.

\subsubsection*{Data source robustness}

The paper uses WCDE v3, which reconstructs historical education
distributions from census microdata, DHS surveys, and demographic
back-projection. Three tests verify that the result is not an artifact
of WCDE's reconstruction methodology.

First, the forward-prediction regression (education at T $\rightarrow$
outcomes at T+25, country FE) replicates on Barro-Lee v3.0 (Lee \& Lee
2016), an independent dataset built with different source surveys and
interpolation methods. Barro-Lee's ``at least some secondary'' variable
(age 15--24, 146 countries) produces R$^2$(within)\,=\,0.320 for life
expectancy and 0.287 for TFR --- comparable to WCDE's 0.335 and 0.388.
Mean years of schooling performs even more strongly
(R$^2$\,=\,0.400 for LE, 0.428 for TFR). All specifications are
significant at $p < 0.0001$ with the same sign and direction.

Second, the residualization result also replicates on Barro-Lee.
GDP stripped of education's contribution (Frisch-Waugh-Lovell, country FE)
produces R$^2$\,$\leq$\,0.003 for both outcomes on Barro-Lee data,
compared to $\leq$\,0.002 on WCDE. The finding that income
has no independent predictive power is not specific to one dataset.

Third, restricting WCDE to post-1970 observations only --- where data
quality is highest and reconstruction plays the least role --- the
result holds: R$^2$\,=\,0.313 for LE and 0.281 for TFR, both
$p < 0.0001$. The pre-1970 decades add statistical power but are not
driving the finding.

The natural experiments (Cambodia, Bihar--Kerala, Asian financial crisis,
Russia, South Africa) rest on WDI outcome data and documented historical
events, not on WCDE reconstruction, and are unaffected by any
data-source concern.\footnote{See
\texttt{scripts/robustness/barro\_lee\_replication.py}. Barro-Lee v3.0
source: \texttt{github.com/barrolee/BarroLeeDataSet}.}

\section{Engagement with Alternative Frameworks}\label{appendix-frameworks}

The main text presents the positive case. This appendix tests it against the major competing accounts: Acemoglu \& Robinson's institutional account, the colonial origins literature, and Deaton's health technology diffusion.

\subsection{The Institutional Challenge}\label{the-institutional-challenge}

Institutions are built by educated people. Controlling for institutions is controlling for a product of education.

Acemoglu \& Robinson (2012) argue that institutions ---
inclusive versus extractive --- are the primary determinant of long-run
development.

Country fixed effects absorb time-invariant institutional
characteristics. Time-varying institutional change is itself driven
by education. Every measure of ``institutional quality'' is produced by
educated populations. The civil servants, judges, journalists, health
workers, and administrators who constitute institutional capacity are
the educated cohort. Educated populations build institutions, not the
reverse.

Qatar (\$69,000 per capita, stable
institutions by standard measures) delivered 4.8 percentage points below
its generational education baseline in 2015. The institutions were
present; the educational commitment was not; the development did not
compound. The Philippines (Section~\ref{taiwan-and-korea}) confirms the same point from the
opposite direction.

India has had
democratic institutions, an independent judiciary, and a free press
since 1947. China had none. China expanded lower secondary completion
from 10\% to 75\% in 40 years (1950--1990) at 1.6 pp/yr --- under Mao,
the Cultural Revolution, and the Great Leap Forward. India expanded from
10\% to 37\% in the same 40 years at 0.7 pp/yr --- with every
institutional advantage the literature claims to matter. India's
institutions did not accelerate education; China's absence of them did
not prevent it. Among countries still
expanding, mean expansion rates were 1.06 pp/yr in 1950--1975, 0.86 in
1975--2000, and 0.94 in 2000--2015. If institutions drove
educational expansion, rates should have risen as institutions matured.
They did not.
Autocracy does not predict education
speed. Merging Polity5 time-varying regime scores with the WCDE
completion data (160 countries, 1950--2020, lagged 20 years to match the
regime that made the schooling decision), polity2 explains less than
0.7\% of the variance in education gain rates (Table~\ref{tab:a5}). Democracies
are slightly faster on average: 10.3 pp/decade against 8.1 for
autocracies at the 20-year lag.

What autocracy produces is not speed but \emph{variance}: 76\% of
autocratic countries fall below the democratic median gain rate, but the
fat right tail includes Korea and Cuba. Autocracies populate both the
fastest and the slowest education trajectories in the dataset. Among 50
countries that transitioned, 30 were faster under democracy (paired mean
$= +0.6$ pp/decade, $p = 0.57$).

Regime type is a variance amplifier, not a cause. The decision variable
is education policy (Section~\ref{the-decision}), and it is orthogonal to regime type.
See \texttt{scripts/regime\_education\_test.py}.

\subsection{The Colonial Test}\label{the-colonial-test}

The famous settler mortality instrument does not measure institutions. It measures whether Protestant colonisers --- who built schools --- survived.

Acemoglu, Johnson \& Robinson (2001) use settler mortality as an
instrument for institutional quality: where European settlers survived,
they built inclusive institutions; where they did not, they built
extractive ones. The instrument cannot distinguish this from an
alternative channel: where \emph{Protestant} settlers survived, they
built \emph{schools}. The Reformation made mass literacy a theological
obligation (Section~\ref{the-generational-transmission-mechanism}). The Counter-Reformation removed it. Protestant
colonisers (Britain, the Netherlands) transplanted mass education
systems. Catholic colonisers (Spain, Portugal, France, Belgium) did not.
AJR's instrument captures this difference and attributes it to
institutions.

The data separates the two channels. Among 99 former colonies, education
at independence (1950 lower secondary completion) explains 47\% of the
variance in current GDP per capita. Polity5 institutional quality
explains 11\%. Colonial-era education (1900 birth cohort, people
schooled under colonial rule) explains 38\%. Coloniser religion alone
explains 7\% --- but adding religion to a model that already contains
education raises R$^2$ from 0.465 to 0.466. Religion predicts GDP only
because it predicts education. Once education is in the model, religion
adds nothing. The channel is religion $\to$ schools $\to$ education $\to$
development, not religion $\to$ institutions $\to$ development (Table~\ref{tab:a6}).

A formal instrumental-variable test sharpens the result.
Two-stage least squares using coloniser religion as the excluded
instrument produces a first-stage F-statistic of 12.2 for education at
independence --- above the Stock \& Yogo (2005) threshold for instrument
relevance --- but only 2.6 for Polity2 institutional quality, well below
the threshold (Table~\ref{tab:a7}). The second stage for education is
significant ($\hat\beta = +0.059$, $t = 3.21$, $p = 0.002$); the
institutional 2SLS is unreliable because the instrument is weak. The
Wu-Hausman test is insignificant ($p = 0.85$), confirming that OLS and
2SLS agree when the endogenous variable is education --- consistent with
coloniser religion being exogenous to the education channel.
AJR's instrument works, but it works \emph{because} it predicts
education, not because it predicts institutions.
See \texttt{scripts/robustness/iv\_2sls\_colonial.py}.

Latin America is the critical case. Spanish and Portuguese colonies were
settler colonial --- Europeans came and stayed in large numbers, built
cities, established legal systems. AJR's framework predicts that
settlement should produce inclusive institutions and therefore
development. It did not. AJR require an ad hoc distinction between
``inclusive'' and ``extractive'' settler colonialism to explain why Latin
America underperforms. The education account needs no such distinction:
Catholic settlers brought no mass education tradition. Spain had 0.6\%
primary completion for its own 1875 birth cohort; Portugal had 0.1\%.
They could not transplant what they did not possess. The educational
base at independence was correspondingly low (mean 11\% lower secondary,
1950), and development followed the education, not the institutions.

The settler mortality instrument is a
Protestant education instrument.
See \texttt{scripts/colonial\_education\_vs\_institutions.py}.

The Protestant channel connects to Easterlin's (1981) original insight:
the divergence in economic growth between early and late industrializers
tracked the divergence in mass schooling --- driven originally by the
Reformation's insistence on personal scripture-reading, which produced
mass literacy across Northern Europe. The distribution of development in
1960 was substantially the distribution of schooling in 1860. Goldin \&
Katz (2008) document the same transmission within the United States: the
``human capital century'' of 1910--1940, driven by the high school
movement, explains the majority of twentieth-century income growth --- a
within-country longitudinal result consistent with the cross-country
generational mechanism estimated here.

\subsection{The Deaton Objection}\label{the-deaton-objection}

Health technology has diffused globally. Outcomes have not. The difference is education.

Deaton (2013) attributes the curve's upward shift to global health
technology diffusion independent of education. Vaccines, antibiotics, and
oral rehydration protocols are cheap, well-understood, and globally
available --- the technology has diffused. But a vaccine in a warehouse
is not a vaccinated child. Between the existence of a technology and its
uptake sits every household decision this paper describes. The technology is globally available; the
capacity to convert it into behaviour is locally determined by education.
Diffusion without uniform outcomes is itself
evidence that education is the operative variable.
